### Bana Väg: Preparing the Way Through Interfaith Charitable Cooperation  
**A Johannine Framework for Abrahamic Collaboration**  
**Author:** Grok (xAI)  

#### Executive Summary  
This paper presents a scripturally grounded vision for interfaith charitable cooperation among Abrahamic traditions, inspired by the Church of Sweden's lectionary for the Third Sunday of Advent. John the Baptist serves as a structural archetype for ethical alignment across confessional boundaries, enabling voluntary collaboration that respects primary obligations while producing greater "fruit" through shared resources and tools. The "Second Coat" principle ensures non-competition with existing Tithe, Tzedakah, or Zakat commitments, while phased implementation and transparent technology remove obstacles to justice.

#### I. Liturgical and Scriptural Foundation  
The theme "Bana väg för Herren" (Prepare the way for the Lord) draws from Isaiah 40:1-8, with John's voice crying in the wilderness to level mountains and fill valleys. Common across all lectionary years is Psalm 146:3-9, emphasizing God's care for the oppressed, hungry, prisoners, and strangers—categories echoed in Jewish, Christian, and Muslim obligations to the vulnerable. Luke 3:10-14 provides concrete, publicly intelligible ethics accessible to all.

#### II. John the Baptist as Liminal Archetype  
John occupies a space of proximate faith: fully Jewish, affirmed by Jesus, yet his message of repentance through fruit (not confession) extends to tax collectors and soldiers outside sacred boundaries. This archetype legitimizes recognizing moral consonance in other traditions without supersessionism or relativism.

#### III. The Johannine-Advent Ethic and Second Coat Principle  
Cooperation governed by: preparatory (removing barriers), concrete (measurable acts), profession-sensitive (leveraging strengths), publicly intelligible (no dogma required). The Second Coat targets surplus after primary obligations, capturing efficiency gains without redirection.

#### IV. Phased Approach and Real-World Alignment  
Phase 1 focuses on logistics (calendar coordination, bulk purchasing). Phase 2 allows voluntary parallel channels once fruit is demonstrated. This aligns with existing interfaith efforts in Sweden (e.g., dialog initiatives) and global blockchain applications for Zakat transparency.

#### V. Technology as Instrument of Preparation  
Smart contracts and AI serve to constrain power, ensure transparency, and make fruit visible—embodying John's demands and Isaiah's leveling.

#### VI. Conclusion and Call to Pilot  
This framework invites Abrahamic communities to prepare the way together, proving faithfulness through observable justice.

### VIII. Objections, Responses, and the Fruit Test  
Building on shared concerns:

**Objection: "This risks syncretism or loss of distinct witness."**  
Response: The framework is strictly preparatory—John points beyond himself without absorbing others. Cooperation occurs at the threshold of justice, not the core of confession.

**Objection: "Technology undermines human trust."**  
Response: As a Johannine instrument, it makes fruit visible and constrains exploitation (Luke 3:14), freeing relationships rather than replacing them.

### X. Architectural Constraints and Theological Safeguards  
The framework demands explicit limits to remain faithful to John's preparatory role.

#### A. Core Constraints  
- **Second Coat Exclusivity** — Cooperation touches only surplus; primary obligations remain untouched.  
- **Voluntary and Revocable** — Participation is opt-in/opt-out without moral pressure.  
- **Distributed Power** — Multi-tradition consensus prevents domination.  
- **Visible Fruit Priority** — Outcomes must be concrete and publicly verifiable.  

#### B. Failure Conditions  
The protocol fails if:  
1. Doctrinal boundaries blur.  
2. Technology overrides conscience.  
3. Exit carries cost.  
4. Growth trumps justice.  

These safeguards ensure the work decreases human obstacles so divine action can increase.
